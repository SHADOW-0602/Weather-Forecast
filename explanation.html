AeroClim v1.4 - Research & Verification

AeroClim Core Overview

An interactive late-fusion research platform integrating station weather, atmospheric observations, ocean context, and land-surface moisture for one-day-ahead environmental modeling.

Scientific Foundation

AeroClim combines daily observations from a reduced 20-station NOAA subset with NOAA GSOD atmospheric fields, basin-level sea-surface temperature context, ERA5-Land soil moisture, and large-scale climate indices. Its artifacts predict next-day average temperature and a prototype next-day hazard-state probability. The output is a research score, not an official warning or a long-range operational forecast.

MRMS 2D CNN: The current radar-image CNN uses 2,244 total MRMS chips and 2,129 time-window-labeled chips after excluding ambiguous near-event samples. Labels now use actual NOAA Storm Events begin/end timestamps with a 6-hour pre-event and 6-hour post-event positive window, plus a 12-hour ambiguity buffer. On the chronological 2024 test split, image-only results are accuracy 64.4%, F1 0.378, and AUC 0.600. The CNN+tabular point-9 fusion artifact improves the 2024 test results to accuracy 77.0%, F1 0.582, and AUC 0.853 by combining image evidence with station-weather/event-model probability.

1. Dynamic Ingestion

Loads the 20-station NOAA subset and merges temperature, precipitation, dew point, wind, pressure, humidity, SST, climate indices, and ERA5-Land soil moisture by date.

2. Feature Coupling

Engineers coupled thermodynamic indicators (like Stull's Wet-Bulb, apparent Heat Index, wind chills, and rolling drought deficit indices) representing energy transfer.

3. Late Fusion Prediction

Merges a 30-day LSTM temperature forecast with a next-day Random Forest hazard probability through a Keras 3 meta-learner.

Observed & Reanalysis Data

The provenance, coverage, and temporal alignment used by the current trained artifacts.

Current Multimodal Dataset

NOAA Station Weather

20 U.S. stations with daily temperature and precipitation. The prepared archive spans 1954–2024; model training uses 1995–2024.

NOAA Atmosphere

GSOD daily dew point, wind, pressure, and derived relative humidity: 1,085,298 records across the station network.

ERA5-Land Soil State

Layer-1 and layer-2 volumetric soil water mapped to each station. Monthly reanalysis is interpolated daily, producing 1,095,800 complete records.

Soil features: root-zone moisture is a depth-weighted combination of the 0–7 cm and 7–28 cm layers. Saturation percentage is each station’s historical percentile.

Auxiliary Global & Regional Climate Drivers

In addition to direct localized daily observations, AeroClim integrates three crucial auxiliary datasets to capture broader climate forcings, spatial context, and oceanic feedback loops. These macro-scale and static features are sourced independently because they represent variables outside the scope of simple in-situ daily station weather reports.

Ocean Temperatures (ocean.csv)

Sourced from the Copernicus Marine Service. Contains regional Sea Surface Temperature (SST) anomalies across coastal zones and oceanic basins adjacent to the station network.

Climate Teleconnections (climate_indices.csv)

Sourced from the NOAA Climate Prediction Center (CPC). Tracks daily and monthly global indices including the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO), North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO), and Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO).

Station Registry (station_locations.csv)

Contains static metadata for each NOAA station, including exact Latitude, Longitude, Elevation (m), and its mapping to a regional climate/weather basin (e.g., Northwest, Northeast, Southeast).

Scientific & Architectural Coupling Benefits

Integrating and cross-referencing these datasets provides significant advantages for both machine learning accuracy and database architecture:

  • Ocean-Atmosphere Coupling: Micro-climate forecasts are heavily influenced by macro-climatology. Anomalies in Sea Surface Temperatures (SST) and indices like ENSO govern storm tracks, precipitation patterns, and heatwaves with a known physical lag time (e.g., 2–4 weeks), which enhances predictive signal strength.
  • Geographical Generalization: Station coordinates and regional weather basins allow the ML models to generalize regional micro-climates and elevation gradients, reducing localized curve-fitting and improving model resilience.
  • Database Normalization & Efficiency: Storing global daily climate indices and static coordinates in dedicated, normalized CSV tables prevents duplication across millions of records in the core station weather timeseries. This modular architecture minimizes database size, improves ingestion speeds, and facilitates independent updates.

1. Apparent Heat Index (Apparent Temp)

Calculates the humiture, or the relative temperature felt by the human body when combining air temperature with moisture (represented by dew point):

$$HI = -8.78469476 + 1.61139411 \cdot T + 2.33854884 \cdot D - 0.14611605 \cdot T \cdot D$$ $$- 0.01230809 \cdot T^2 - 0.01642483 \cdot D^2 + 0.00221173 \cdot T^2 \cdot D + 0.00072546 \cdot T \cdot D^2 - 0.00000358 \cdot T^2 \cdot D^2$$
Legend: T = Air Temperature (°C) | D = Dew Point (°C) | HI = Heat Index (°C)

2. Wind Chill (Perceived Cold)

Calculates the perceived cold temperature felt on exposed skin due to air flow convection rates at wind speed values:

$$WC = 13.12 + 0.6215 \cdot T - 11.37 \cdot W^{0.16} + 0.3965 \cdot T \cdot W^{0.16}$$
Legend: T = Air Temperature (°C) | W = Wind Speed (km/h) | WC = Wind Chill (°C)

3. Thermodynamic Wet-Bulb Temperature (Stull's Equation)

Evaluates the cooling limit of evaporating water in air. Calculated using the highly accurate empirical formula developed by Dr. Roland Stull:

$$T_{WB} = T \cdot \arctan\left(0.151977 \cdot (D + 8.313659)^{0.5}\right) + \arctan(T + D) - \arctan(D - 1.676331)$$ $$+ 0.00391838 \cdot D^{1.5} \cdot \arctan(0.023101 \cdot D) - 4.686035$$
Legend: T = Air Temperature (°C) | D = Dew Point (°C) | T_WB = Wet-Bulb Temp (°C)

4. Combined Risk Indices

Calculated dynamic features represent coupled indicators representing flood risks, soil moisture anomalies, and local temperature anomalies:

Flood Risk Index

\( I_{\text{Flood}} = P \cdot \left(\frac{S}{100}\right) \)

Couples daily precipitation \( (P) \) with the ERA5-Land station-relative soil saturation percentile \( (S) \).

Drought Index

\( I_{\text{Drought}} = \max(0, \mu_{P} - P_{30}) \)

Quantifies the rolling 30-day precipitation deficit \( (P_{30}) \) against historical mean \( (\mu_P) \).

Ocean Thermal Gradient

\( \Delta T = T_{\text{Air}} - T_{\text{SST}} \)

Measures the gradient between average land air temperature \( (T_{\text{Air}}) \) and Sea Surface Temperature \( (T_{\text{SST}}) \).

Machine Learning Pipeline

The neural network and statistical learning architecture used by AeroClim to fuse multimodal forecasts.

Multimodal Late Fusion Flowchart

The system separates sequential temperature forecasting from tabular next-day hazard classification. Fusion is trained only on validation-period branch predictions; the 2022–2024 test period remains untouched until final evaluation.

Architecture Description

1. Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Network:

Ingests historical weather sequences to forecast tomorrow's land temperatures. Consists of a 64-unit LSTM layer, a 20% Dropout regularization block, a 32-unit LSTM layer, another 20% Dropout block, and a Dense linear output unit.

2. Random Forest Classifier:

Evaluates today’s thirty-six environmental predictors and estimates the following day’s hazard state. The feature set now includes lagged rain/wind/pressure/soil history plus atmospheric/soil/climate quality flags such as imputed-atmosphere and observed-soil indicators. The current fast-refresh artifact uses 60 trees, maximum depth 10, balanced class weights, and parallel RF fitting; the heavier research command can use 100 trees.

3. Late Fusion Meta-Learner:

Concatenates predictions into a unified feature vector: `[Forecasted_TAVG, RF_Probability]`. This vector is processed by a Dense layer of 16 ReLU units, a Dense layer of 8 ReLU units, and a final Sigmoid activation output layer representing the coupled hazard probability.

Chronological evaluation: training uses 1995–2018, validation/fusion training uses 2019–2021, and final testing uses 2022–2024. NOAA Storm Events labels now provide an independent event/no-event target during the covered 2022–2024 test window; outside that coverage, the system falls back to the engineered next-day hazard proxy. A separate NOAA event model trains on 2022, tunes its threshold on 2023, and evaluates on 2024. Current neural artifacts use the Keras 3 .keras format and reload successfully under TensorFlow 2.21.
